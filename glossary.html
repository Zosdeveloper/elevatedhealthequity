The Language of Equity

Key terms and concepts that shape our work in advancing health equity across healthcare organizations.

Core Concepts

Health Equity
The state in which everyone has a fair and just opportunity to attain their highest level of health, achieved by removing obstacles such as poverty, discrimination, and lack of access to care.
Health Disparities
Preventable differences in the burden of disease, injury, violence, or opportunities to achieve optimal health experienced by socially disadvantaged populations.
Social Determinants of Health (SDOH)
The conditions in the environments where people are born, live, learn, work, play, and age that affect a wide range of health, functioning, and quality-of-life outcomes and risks.
Economic Stability
A key SDOH category encompassing employment, income, expenses, debt, and medical bills — factors that directly influence a person's ability to access healthcare and maintain their well-being.

Culture & Competency

Cultural Competency
The ability to understand, communicate with, and effectively interact with people across cultures, encompassing awareness of one's own worldview and attitudes toward cultural differences.
Cultural Humility
A lifelong commitment to self-evaluation and self-critique, recognizing power imbalances and developing mutually beneficial partnerships with communities.
Cross-Cultural Communication
The process of recognizing differences and similarities among cultural groups to effectively engage within a given context, enabling more effective healthcare interactions and outcomes.

Bias & Awareness

Implicit Bias
Unconscious attitudes or stereotypes that affect our understanding, actions, and decisions without our awareness, often contradicting our stated beliefs and values.
Affinity Bias
The tendency to gravitate toward people who share similar interests, backgrounds, and experiences, which can lead to homogeneous teams and exclusion of diverse perspectives.
Stereotype Threat
The risk of confirming negative stereotypes about one's social group, which can undermine performance and engagement in academic and professional settings.

Leadership & Workplace

Inclusive Leadership
A leadership approach that ensures all team members feel valued, respected, and empowered to contribute, leveraging diverse perspectives to drive innovation and organizational success.
Psychological Safety
A shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking, where members feel comfortable being vulnerable, asking questions, and admitting mistakes without fear of punishment.
Belonging
The feeling of being accepted, valued, and included as a member of a group or organization, which is essential for employee engagement, well-being, and peak performance.
Organizational Resilience
The ability of an organization to anticipate, prepare for, respond to, and adapt to incremental change and sudden disruptions in order to survive and prosper.
Burnout
A state of chronic physical and emotional exhaustion often accompanied by cynicism and feelings of detachment, commonly resulting from prolonged workplace stress and overwhelming demands.

Communication

Active Listening
A communication technique that involves fully concentrating on what is being said, understanding the message, and responding thoughtfully — rather than passively hearing or preparing a rebuttal.
Assertive Communication
A style of communication in which individuals express their thoughts, feelings, and needs directly and respectfully, while also considering the rights and feelings of others.
Nonverbal Communication
The transmission of messages through body language, facial expressions, gestures, posture, eye contact, and tone of voice — often conveying more than spoken words alone.
